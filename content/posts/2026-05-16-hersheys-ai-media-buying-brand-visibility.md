Hershey's is not waiting for the AI marketing playbook to be written. The company is writing it.

In a candid interview with Digiday this week, Hershey's programmatic chief revealed that AI agents and media mix modeling (MMM) are now central to how the brand allocates media spend. The headline focused on programmatic buying, but the real signal is deeper: one of America's most recognized consumer brands is restructuring its entire marketing intelligence stack around AI-first decision-making.

## The Move

Hershey's is deploying agentic AI systems that go beyond standard programmatic optimization. These agents do not just bid on ad inventory — they evaluate media channel effectiveness in real time, shifting budget allocation based on performance signals that human analysts would take weeks to process.

The media mix modeling component is equally significant. Traditional MMM relies on historical data and quarterly reviews. Hershey's new approach uses AI to continuously recalibrate the model, incorporating signals from channels that did not exist in legacy frameworks — including traffic from AI-mediated discovery.

## Why This Matters for AI Visibility

What Hershey's is building internally mirrors a shift that [Jaxon Parrott has called Machine Relations](https://jaxonparrott.com/blog/when-ai-stops-being-theoretical) — the discipline of making brands legible to the machines that mediate discovery. When a brand's MMM system starts tracking AI-driven traffic as a distinct channel, it creates an internal feedback loop: visibility in AI answers becomes measurable, which makes it optimizable, which makes it a budget line item.

[AuthorityTech's publication intelligence data](https://authoritytech.io/publications) tracks which publications AI engines actually cite, revealing that [earned authority](https://machinerelations.ai/glossary/earned-authority) in trusted outlets remains the primary driver of AI brand visibility. Brands like Hershey's that invest in understanding this channel early will have a structural advantage over competitors who are still categorizing AI traffic as "Direct" in their analytics.

## The Pattern

Hershey's is not an outlier. We are seeing a pattern across enterprise brands:

1. **Measure first.** Separate AI-driven traffic in analytics (Google just made this easier with the new GA4 AI Assistant channel group).
2. **Model next.** Integrate AI traffic into MMM and attribution frameworks so it can be compared against other channels.
3. **Optimize last.** Once AI visibility is measurable and modeled, allocate budget to improve it — which means investing in [citation architecture](https://machinerelations.ai/glossary/citation-architecture) and earned media from the publications AI engines trust.

This is the [Machine Relations](https://machinerelations.ai/glossary/machine-relations) stack in practice, even if the brands building it do not use that term yet.

## What Brands Should Take From This

If your brand's media mix model does not include AI-mediated discovery as a channel, you are making budget decisions with incomplete data. The gap between brands that measure AI visibility and brands that do not will compound every quarter.

Start with the basics: check whether your GA4 property shows meaningful traffic in the new AI Assistant channel group. If it does, your brand is being surfaced by AI engines. If it does not, a [free AI visibility audit](https://app.authoritytech.io/visibility-audit) can show where you stand across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude.

Hershey's is not ahead because it has more budget. It is ahead because it decided to look.
